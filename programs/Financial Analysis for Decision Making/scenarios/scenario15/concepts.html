Concepts

Cash Flow Management – Techniques and strategies to manage a company's cash inflows and outflows to maintain liquidity and operational stability.

1. Accounts Receivable Management (Days Sales Outstanding - DSO)

Accounts Receivable Management focuses on efficiently managing the money owed by customers to ensure timely cash inflows. Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) is a key metric that measures the average number of days it takes to collect payment after a sale has been made.

Establish stringent credit terms to minimize delays in payments. Continuously track DSO to identify trends and take corrective actions promptly.

  • DSO = (Accounts Receivable / Total Credit Sales) × Number of Days
  • Where Number of Days is typically 365 for annual calculation.

Use digital invoicing systems to ensure timely and accurate billing. Engage with customers early on to follow up on outstanding payments.

Application:

  • Accounts Receivable: INR 30 Lakhs (average delay of 30 days)
  • Total Credit Sales: INR 1,00,00,000 annually
  • DSO Calculation: DSO = (30,00,000 / 1,00,00,000) × 365 ≈ 109.5 days
  • Target DSO: Reduce to 60 days to improve cash flow.
  • Target Reduction: Current DSO − Desired DSO = 109.5 − 60 = 49.5 days

2. Cash Flow Forecasting

Cash Flow Forecasting involves predicting future cash inflows and outflows to ensure the company maintains adequate liquidity to meet its obligations. It is essential for maintaining operational stability, especially during periods of cost-cutting and efficiency improvements. Accurate forecasting helps in proactive decision-making and financial planning.

  • Develop multiple cash flow scenarios (best-case, worst-case) to prepare for uncertainties.
  • Update forecasts regularly with actual performance data to improve accuracy.
  • Net Cash Flow = Total Cash Inflows − Total Cash Outflows
  • Short-Term Forecast:
    • Inflows: Accelerated receivables through early payment discounts
    • Outflows: Operational expenses, payroll, supplier payments
  • Manage inventory levels, receivables, and payables efficiently to improve cash flow. Positive Operating Cash Flow (OCF) indicates healthy cash generation from operations.
  • Operating Cash Flow (OCF) = Net Income + Non-Cash Expenses − Increase in Working Capital
  • Where Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities
  • Free Cash Flow (FCF) = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures

Measurement of Cash Flow

  • Focus on increasing operating cash flow through efficient receivables management and cost control.
  • Reduce inventory levels and negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers to shorten the CCC.
  • Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) = Days Sales Outstanding + Days Inventory Outstanding − Days Payable Outstanding
  • Use historical ratio data to identify improvements or deteriorations in cash flow management.
  • Compare ratios against industry standards to gauge performance and identify areas for improvement.

Example Forecast:

  • Current Cash Reserves: INR 10 Lakhs
  • Expected Inflows from Reduced DSO: INR 40 Lakhs
  • Expected Outflows: INR 30 Lakhs
  • Net Cash Flow: INR 10 Lakhs + INR 40 Lakhs - INR 30 Lakhs = INR 20 Lakhs

3. Liquidity Ratios (Current Ratio and Quick Ratio)

Liquidity Ratios assess a company's ability to meet its short-term obligations. The Current Ratio and Quick Ratio are essential indicators of financial health and liquidity.

  • Negotiate better payment terms with suppliers to lower current liabilities.
  • Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
  • Reduce excess inventory to improve the Quick Ratio without affecting production.
  • Quick Ratio = (Current Assets − Inventory) / Current Liabilities

Target Ratios:

  • Current Ratio: ≥ 1.5
  • Quick Ratio: ≥ 1.0

Aim to increase inventory turnover to reach the target Quick Ratio.

Application:

  • Current Assets: INR 50 Lakhs
  • Current Liabilities: INR 40 Lakhs
  • Inventory: INR 20 Lakhs
  • Current Ratio: 50,00,000 / 40,00,000 = 1.25
  • Quick Ratio: (50,00,000 − 20,00,000) / 40,00,000 = 0.75
