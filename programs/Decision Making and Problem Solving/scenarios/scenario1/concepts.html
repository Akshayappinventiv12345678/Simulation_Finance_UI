Scenario 1: Bidding for New Projects Concepts

Concepts

To effectively navigate the scenario where GlobalFusion Industries must decide on a commercial opportunity, participants should be well-versed in the following concepts. These concepts, drawn from the Decision Making and Problem-Solving Program, are essential for leaders to apply in their daily corporate activities.

1. SWOT Analysis (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats)

A strategic tool to evaluate internal strengths and weaknesses, and external opportunities and threats affecting organizational success.

  • Focus on the most critical strengths and threats that can significantly impact the business.
  • Bidding strategies should be aligned with the company's strengths and market opportunities.
  • Weaknesses and threats can be mitigated by developing targeted action plans.

Components:

  • Strengths: Internal advantages (e.g., proprietary technology, strong brand)
  • Weaknesses: Internal limitations (e.g., high turnover, outdated systems)
  • Opportunities: External possibilities (e.g., emerging markets, new technologies)
  • Threats: External challenges (e.g., new competitors, regulatory changes)

2. Cost Benefit Analysis

Cost Benefit Analysis involves comparing the total expected costs of an initiative against the total expected benefits to determine its financial viability and strategic value.

  • Include all direct and indirect costs such as maintenance, training, and downtime during installation.
  • Estimate tangible benefits like increased production efficiency, reduced defect rates, and higher sales.
  • Assess how changes in key assumptions (e.g., cost savings, revenue growth) affect the CBA outcomes.

Key Formulas:

  • Cost-Benefit Ratio = Total Benefits / Total Costs
  • CBA Ratio > 1 ⇒ Beneficial Investment
  • Net Benefit = Total Benefits − Total Costs

Executives use cost-benefit analysis to prioritize projects with the highest return on cost. Options with a ratio significantly greater than 1 are preferred.

Application:

  • Costs: Calculate bid preparation expenses (e.g., INR 1,50,000) and administrative costs for joint bids (e.g., INR 75,000).
  • Benefits: Estimate potential revenue from successful bids (e.g., INR 50,00,000 individually or INR 25,00,000 when shared).
  • Decision-Making: Determine the most financially viable option between bidding alone or partnering.

3. Risk Management and Mitigation

Risk Assessment is the process of identifying, assessing, and developing plans to manage potential risks. Mitigation Strategies are then developed to minimize the impact of these risks on the project’s success.

  • Executives implement risk assessment tools before committing to a process.
  • They establish risk mitigation strategies to minimize potential losses and regularly monitor risks throughout the bidding and project execution phases.

Application:

  • Risk Identification: Recognize risks like not winning the bid, overrunning costs, or conflicts in partnerships.
  • Risk Score = Probability × Impact
  • Expected Monetary Value (EMV): ∑ (Probability of Risk × Impact of Risk)
  • Mitigation Plans: Develop strategies such as contingency budgets, clear partnership agreements, or alternative opportunities.

Identified Risks:

  • Technological Obsolescence: High probability, high impact.
  • Market Demand Fluctuations: Medium probability, high impact.
  • Supplier Reliability: Low probability, medium impact.

Risk Mitigation Strategies:

  • Technological Obsolescence: Invest in scalable and upgradable machinery.
  • Market Demand Fluctuations: Diversify product lines to reduce dependency on a single market segment.
  • Supplier Reliability: Establish multiple supplier relationships to ensure continuity.
